### **Bernoulli’s Equation**

* Published by **Daniel Bernoulli** in 1738.

* Describes the relationship between **pressure**, **velocity**, and **elevation** in a fluid.

* Equation (pressure form):
  [
  P + \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 + \rho g H = \text{constant along a streamline}
  ]
  Where:

  * (P) = static pressure
  * (\frac{1}{2}\rho V^2) = dynamic pressure (kinetic energy per unit volume)
  * (\rho g H) = hydrostatic pressure (potential energy per unit volume)

* Can also be expressed in **head form** or **energy form**.

* Essentially a **conservation of energy** statement along a streamline.

---

### **Limitations**

* Only valid along a streamline.
* Assumes:

  * Laminar, steady flow
  * Inviscid fluid (negligible viscosity)
  * Incompressible fluid

---

### **Example: Flow Through a Pipe**

1. Consider a pipe with a diameter change: large → small.
2. Using **continuity equation** (( \rho A V = \text{constant} )) to relate velocities:

   * Smaller cross-section → higher velocity.
3. Apply Bernoulli’s equation → pressure decreases as velocity increases.

---

### **Bernoulli’s Principle**

* States: **Increase in fluid velocity → decrease in pressure** (for horizontal flow).
* Explains:

  * **Airplane wings (lift):** Faster flow over the top creates low pressure, generating lift.
  * **Bunsen burners:** High-velocity gas draws in air for combustion.

---

### **Flow Measurement Devices**

1. **Pitot-Static Tube**

   * Measures fluid velocity, often in aircraft.
   * Stagnation point → fluid velocity zero → measures **stagnation pressure**.
   * Outer tube measures **static pressure** → velocity calculated from difference.

2. **Venturi Meter**

   * Measures flow rate by pressure drop across converging section of pipe.
   * Flow rate equation derived from Bernoulli + continuity equation.
   * No moving parts; diverging section prevents flow separation.

---

### **Practical Example: Beer Keg**

* Gravity-fed keg with tap at bottom.
* Atmospheric pressure at top and bottom → static pressures cancel.
* Assume fluid initially at rest at the top.
* Bernoulli’s equation gives beer velocity at tap:
  [
  V = \sqrt{2 g H}
  ]

* Assumptions must hold for valid application.
* Adapted equations exist for unsteady or compressible flows.
